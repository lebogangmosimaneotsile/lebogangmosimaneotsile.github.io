The Changing of the Guard

On Pep Guardiola, the end of an era, and using football to measure how much of your life has passed

Football is one of the ways I measure how old I am getting. The players I grew up watching, the managers I grew up arguing about, the seasons that bled into each other until suddenly a decade had passed and I genuinely had not noticed. I really had not noticed that Pep Guardiola had been at Manchester City for ten years until this weekend made it impossible to ignore.

This past weekend, the 2025-26 Premier League season ended. Arsenal won the title. Pep managed his last City game. Salah and Robertson played their last games for Liverpool. Bernardo Silva and John Stones said goodbye to City. Seamus Coleman left Everton. Kyle Walker is at Burnley in the Championship now, which still does not feel real. And I sat with all of it and felt, very clearly, that something had closed. Not ended badly. Just closed. The way a chapter closes. Completely, and without apology.

What Pep actually did

To understand why his departure feels significant beyond the trophies, you have to understand what Pep Guardiola actually changed. When he arrived in English football in 2016, the Premier League had a certain grammar. Physical. Direct. Built around the number nine, the big targetman striker who held up play and finished chances. It was a grammar that had served the league well for decades and nobody was seriously questioning it.

Pep had already questioned it at Barcelona, where he popularised the false nine, the idea of removing the traditional striker entirely and using a fluid, intelligent attacker to occupy that space without being fixed to it. Messi was the most famous expression of this idea, but the idea itself was Pep's gift to the game. He brought that thinking to Munich, adapted it, won with it, then brought it to Manchester and essentially rewired what English football believed was possible.

Six league titles. Ten years. Twenty major trophies in total. But the numbers are almost beside the point. What Pep leaves behind is not just silverware. It is a way of thinking about football that has become the default language of the sport at the highest level. High press. Positional play. The goalkeeper as the first line of attack. The fullback inverting into midfield. These were radical ideas when he first deployed them. They are now simply how good football is coached. He did not just win. He changed what winning looked like.

One could reasonably argue he is the single greatest architect of the modern game. Not the only influence, but the most pervasive one. And the Premier League, the most watched league in the world, was where that influence reached its fullest expression.

The disciples

Here is what I find most poetic about this particular moment. Pep leaves, and waiting to fill the space he leaves behind are three managers who learned the game either directly under him or in the era he shaped. Mikel Arteta was Pep's assistant at City before taking the Arsenal job. Enzo Maresca worked under him at City as well. And while Xabi Alonso was not formally Pep's disciple, Pep was his manager at Bayern Munich, and Alonso has spent his coaching career in clear conversation with that philosophy. The DNA runs through all three of them.

The era does not end so much as it transforms. Pep steps back, and the Premier League's next chapter is written by men shaped by everything he built. There is something almost mythological about that. The teacher leaves, and the students take the chairs. The language he invented is now being spoken by the next generation as though it is their mother tongue. Because for them, it is.

He did not just win. He changed what winning looked like. And now the men shaped by that are the ones who will write the next chapter.

The players I grew up watching

This is where it gets personal, and I want to be honest about the limits of what I can claim here. Arteta and Xabi Alonso are names I know well, figures whose careers I am aware of. But if I am being truthful, I was not really watching football during their playing days in the Premier League. I cannot claim them as mine in that way.

Michael Carrick, though. I was there for Michael Carrick. I watched him at Manchester United. Consistently underrated, consistently essential. The quiet fulcrum of those title-winning teams, the player opposition fans never quite respected until he was not there anymore. He is a manager now. At Manchester United of all places, permanent head coach as of this week, having come in as interim in January and turned the whole season around. And something about seeing him in a dugout rather than in the middle of a Manchester United midfield does something to my sense of time.

Because here is what happened this weekend. They played the highlight packages, the tribute reels, the then-and-now comparisons. Pep in 2016 arriving at the Etihad. Salah in his first season at Anfield. Robertson when he first pulled on that Liverpool shirt. And I looked at them, and I looked at the difference in how they look between then and now, and I had to sit with it for a moment. These people have gotten older. Visibly, undeniably older. And I had not quite clocked it because it happened progressively, one season into the next, nothing dramatic enough in any single moment to make you stop and take stock. But put the images side by side and it lands differently. Ten years has passed. Ten years has actually, genuinely, flown by.

Which means I have to reckon with something. If the players I grew up watching are now the managers, and the managers I grew up watching are now stepping away, then I am not a kid anymore. That sounds obvious. It is obvious. But football has a way of making you feel like time is standing still because the game itself never stops. There is always a next season, always a next match, always a reason to believe the best is still coming. And then one weekend it just ends. Pep says goodbye, Salah and Robertson walk off the Anfield pitch for the last time, and you sit with the quiet and understand that time was moving the whole time. You just were not paying attention in the right way.

The end of an era, properly understood

Virgil van Dijk is, to my mind, the last man standing from that generation in any meaningful sense. Still at Liverpool, still commanding. But when he goes, and he will, the thread will be fully cut. Klopp has already left. Trent Alexander-Arnold has already left. De Bruyne has left. Henderson is at Brentford. Kyle Walker is in the Championship. The core of what made the last decade of Premier League football feel like a genuine golden age, two great managers pushing each other to extraordinary heights season after season, is gone.

Arsenal winning the title this season felt right, in a strange way. And I say that as someone who is, if anything, an Arsenal hater — the biggest. But it felt like the correct punctuation at the end of a very long sentence. A new team winning, a new era beginning, old certainties dissolving. Pep leaving was the final nail in the coffin. When the best manager in the world decides it is his time to go, something is genuinely over.

I am sad about it, but not really. Things ending is not the same as things going wrong. The Pep era was extraordinary and it ran its full course and it leaves the game richer than it found it. That is as good as it gets. But I am aware of it in a way that feels different to normal football feelings. It feels like the closing of something I did not know I was holding on to.

Maybe that is just what it feels like to grow up. You use football to track it because football never pretends time is not passing. The seasons come and the seasons end. Players arrive and players leave. And every so often a manager says goodbye, and you realise that the years you spent watching him were, quietly, some of the best years of the sport you love.

Written on 25 May 2026, the day after the 2025-26 Premier League season ended. Pep Guardiola managed his last Manchester City game after ten years and twenty major trophies. Mohamed Salah and Andy Robertson played their final games for Liverpool after nine years each. Arsenal were crowned Premier League champions. Bernardo Silva, John Stones, Kyle Walker and Seamus Coleman also departed their clubs.
