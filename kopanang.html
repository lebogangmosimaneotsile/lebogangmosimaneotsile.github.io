Kopanang

A Digital Community for Faith Groups

Role

UX Research · UI Design · Concept

Timeline

Status

Wireframe stage — hi-fi in progress

This project began as an academic research proposal in 2022, during my Psychology and Sociology degree at the University of Botswana, and was developed into a UX design concept in 2025. The proposal was assessed as coursework but never carried through to actual data collection — the personas and design rationale below are informed by my own lived experience and the proposal's research design, not by completed interviews.

The Problem

When places of worship closed, something else closed with them.

When governments around the world closed places of worship in 2020, the immediate conversation was about access to services. How do we keep people connected to their faith? The answer came quickly — stream it. Record it. Put it on YouTube, Facebook, Apple Podcasts. And it worked, technically. People could still hear the sermon.

But something else was lost that nobody had designed a solution for.

The conversation after the service — standing outside the church in the sun, catching up with someone you hadn't seen in two weeks. The ability to walk into an empty church on a Wednesday afternoon, sit in the quiet, and just pray. The WhatsApp message from your group leader reminding you that your ministry is leading Mass this Sunday. The priest who knew you were struggling and could see it on your face when you walked in.

None of that streamed.

Drawing on my own experience and the questions the original proposal was built to answer, three unmet needs seemed clearest to me: individual spiritual space; peer connection within age groups; and small group coordination and belonging. Existing workarounds solved content delivery. Kopanang was designed to solve connection.

Research Foundation

A real proposal. An unfinished study.

Most app concepts start with an assumption. This one started with an academic proposal — though it's important to say upfront that the proposal was never carried through to actual interviews.

In 2022, as part of my studies in Psychology and Sociology at the University of Botswana, I designed a qualitative research study titled "The Effect COVID-19 Has Had on Religious Practices in Botswana." The proposal was built around a sample of 72 participants across 6 faith groups in Gaborone, using semi-structured in-depth interviews. It was submitted and assessed as coursework. The interviews themselves were never conducted.

The study was grounded in phenomenological research design — the goal was never to measure statistics, but to understand lived experience. That intention still shapes how I think about this project, even though the data behind it doesn't exist.

These numbers describe the study's intended design, not completed data collection.

Working Assumptions Behind the Design

These aren't research findings — the interviews never happened. They're informed assumptions, shaped by the proposal's research questions, by general literature on religion and COVID-19, and by my own experience as a lifelong member of a faith community in Botswana.

Content is not community

It's reasonable to assume that streamed services kept faith technically accessible without replacing what it feels like to belong to a congregation. Receiving content passively isn't the same as being known by the people around you.

Age shapes how people experience community

It's likely that younger people — particularly those between 18 and 35 — lost peer connection most acutely. The informal lateral bonds between people in the same life stage would have been severed, with no digital workaround replacing them.

Religious leaders probably lost something too

Leaders likely didn't just struggle professionally — for many, pastoral interaction is deeply personal. The counselling sessions, the familiar faces, the ability to read a congregation — losing that would be isolating in ways that go beyond the role itself.

The elderly were probably most vulnerable, and least served

Older congregants likely rely most heavily on physical presence and human interaction. Digital workarounds require technological fluency that many wouldn't have had — and it's unlikely anyone was designing with them specifically in mind.

A note on positionality: I am not a neutral observer of this topic. I grew up in the Roman Catholic Church in Botswana. The assumptions above are informed by years of personal experience — knowing what it feels like to belong to a faith community, and what it feels like when that community goes quiet. In UX, we call this being a subject matter expert and a user simultaneously. I'd call it having skin in the game.

This personal grounding is part of why I felt confident building informed assumptions for this concept even without completed interview data — but it isn't a substitute for actually asking people, which is the most important next step for this project.

User Groups

Four people. Four very different losses.

Kopanang was designed for four distinct groups. They all belong to the same faith communities, but they experience those communities — and the loss of them — very differently.

These personas are built from the proposal's research design and my own experience, not from interview transcripts — there are none yet. They represent informed hypotheses about how different people in the same faith community might experience this loss differently.

The Young Adult

18–35

↓

She's 26, grew up in the Catholic Church, knows everyone in her age group at her parish. She leads Mass some Sundays with her ministry group, shows up for cleaning rosters, attends the occasional youth meeting. Church for her isn't just spiritual — it's social infrastructure. It's where she sees people her own age navigating the same things she is: early careers, relationships, figuring out who they are outside of their parents' house.

When COVID hit, the sermon moved online. Her social infrastructure didn't. WhatsApp groups helped with announcements but couldn't replace the conversation outside the church gates after Mass.

What she needs from Kopanang: Peer connection, age-filtered community, and group coordination for her ministry.

The Established Adult

36–50

↓

He's in his early forties, has been a member of his congregation for over a decade. He's less interested in peer socialising and more invested in the rhythms and rituals of his faith community — knowing what's happening, staying connected to his church family, maintaining the sense of continuity that his faith gives him.

During COVID, what he lost most was routine. The anchor points of his week — Sunday service, midweek prayer, greeting familiar faces — disappeared without warning.

What he needs from Kopanang: Reliable community updates, a space that reflects the maturity of his faith journey, and connection without the noise of younger-skewing social platforms.

The Elder

50+

↓

She's in her late sixties. She has attended the same church for thirty years. She knows the priest by name, she knows which pew she sits in, she knows the faces of every family in her section of the congregation. Her faith is inseparable from her physical presence in that building.

When the church closed, she lost more than a service. She lost her primary source of human contact outside her household. She is the user Kopanang has not yet fully solved for — and that's an honest acknowledgement. The current wireframes don't accommodate her digital literacy needs. A simplified mode is planned for future iterations.

What she needs from Kopanang: Simplicity, familiarity, and the feeling that someone thought about her when they built this.

The Religious Leader

Pastoral role

↓

He's been a priest for fifteen years. His life is structured around his congregation — not just professionally, but personally. COVID took his congregation away from him at the same time it took them away from each other. He couldn't see faces. He couldn't read the room. The person he had been supporting through a difficult season stopped answering their phone. He had no way in.

What often goes unsaid is that for many religious leaders, especially those who have given their lives to a calling that limits their personal social world, pastoral interaction is deeply sustaining. The isolation of COVID wasn't just a professional challenge. It was a personal one.

What he needs from Kopanang: A way to maintain presence with his congregation, reach people who have gone quiet, and feel connected to the community he has dedicated his life to.

Design Decisions

Three features. Each rooted in an assumption.

Every screen in Kopanang exists because of a specific, named assumption about what was lost. None of these have been validated by real interviews yet.

Feature 01

The Sanctuary
For the person who just needs to be still

The Assumption

One of the quieter assumptions behind this design is how much people likely missed the ability to simply enter a church. Not for a service. Not for a meeting. Just to sit, pray, and leave. That act — private, unstructured, personal — has no obvious digital equivalent.

The Decision

The Sanctuary is a private, individual space within Kopanang. It is deliberately minimal — a candle, a prompt to write a private prayer, and a scripture passage at the bottom of the screen. Nothing is social here. Nothing is shared. The candle interaction grounds the experience in something familiar and symbolic, echoing the physical act of lighting a candle in church before prayer. The typography is serif, unhurried. The palette is white and soft. The screen asks nothing of the user except presence.

What's Still Unresolved

The prayer input is currently private, but future iterations should clarify whether prayers are stored locally, synced to a personal account, or never saved at all. For a feature this intimate, the data handling needs to be explicitly communicated to users — not buried in a privacy policy. Trust is the entire foundation of this screen.

The Sanctuary — a minimal, private space for individual prayer. Nothing is social here.

Feature 02

The Plaza
For the person who misses the conversation after Mass

The Assumption

The post-service catch-up — standing outside in the sun, talking to people you hadn't seen in a week — seems like one of the most likely losses, based on the proposal's framing and my own experience. It was informal, unscheduled, and irreplaceable. And a 26-year-old and a 70-year-old probably don't lose the same thing when their church closes — they don't need the same thing to feel connected again.

The Decision

The Plaza is a post-service social space, structured by age group — 18–25, 26–35, 36–50, and 50+. The age filter isn't about exclusion. It's about relevance. In every physical church community, people naturally cluster by life stage. The region filter extends this beyond a single congregation — allowing users to connect with people of the same faith and age group across Botswana and eventually across Sub-Saharan Africa.

What's Still Unresolved

The 50+ room exists in the wireframe but hasn't been designed with that user group's needs in mind. A simplified mode is planned for future iterations — one that reduces interface complexity without reducing functionality. Designing for lower digital literacy isn't a cosmetic adjustment. It requires a fundamentally different interaction model.

The Plaza — post-service community space structured by age group, so every conversation is actually relevant.

Feature 03

The Village
For the person who needs to know their group hasn't forgotten them

The Assumption

Within every faith community, there are smaller groups operating beneath the surface — ministries, youth groups, choirs, cleaning rosters, prayer circles. It's reasonable to assume that during COVID, they lost their coordination infrastructure entirely — that leaders couldn't maintain presence, and members didn't know what was happening.

The Decision

The Village is a group coordination hub. Each group has its own space showing upcoming responsibilities, events, and a chat preview. The design is functional rather than social — it prioritises information and coordination over conversation, because what these groups most likely needed wasn't another place to chat. It was clarity. Knowing when they were leading Mass. Knowing when the cleaning roster was. Knowing their group was still there.

What's Still Unresolved

The Village currently shows one group hub. In practice, many congregants belong to multiple groups simultaneously — a youth ministry and a choir, for example. How a user navigates between multiple group memberships needs to be worked out in the next iteration. There's also a question of moderation — who administers a group, who can post events, and how that hierarchy maps onto existing church structures.

The Village — a group coordination hub showing upcoming responsibilities, events, and a chat preview.

Limitations

Honest about what's missing.

The research was never conducted. This is the most significant limitation, and the one that matters most: the proposal was real and the methodology was sound, but the actual interviews never happened. Every persona, assumption, and design decision above is an informed projection, not a validated result. The single most valuable next step for this project is to actually run the study using the methodology and interview questions I wrote in 2022.
Only three screens exist. The wireframes cover the core feature areas but leave significant parts of the user journey undesigned — onboarding, navigation, empty states, error handling, and the overall information architecture are all outstanding.
The elderly user group is underserved. The 50+ room exists in The Plaza, but the current design makes no accommodation for lower digital literacy. This is the most significant gap between the design's assumptions and the design response. It needs to be addressed, not deferred indefinitely.
If conducted, the research would be qualitative and Gaborone-specific. The proposal was built around 72 participants across 6 faith groups in one city — rich in depth but not statistically generalisable by design. Expanding the research base, particularly across different regions of Botswana and into the broader Sub-Saharan African context, would strengthen the design foundation considerably.

Next Steps

What comes next.

  1. Actually conduct the interviews. Use the original 2022 proposal — the same sampling plan, the same interview questions for congregants and religious leaders — and either confirm or revise the assumptions the current design is built on. This is the step that would turn Kopanang from a concept into a research-backed project.
  2. Design a simplified mode for elderly users — dedicated research with that user group, followed by a parallel design track that reduces interface complexity without reducing dignity or functionality.
  3. Map the full user journey — onboarding, navigation between the three spaces, notification logic, and account management all need to be designed before this concept can be properly evaluated as a product.
  4. Move to high-fidelity — the wireframes have established the structure. The next sprint will focus on visual identity: colour, typography, component system, and iconography, grounded in the calm, spiritual tone the product demands.
  5. Conduct usability testing — the design has been informed by assumption, but it hasn't yet been tested by users. Before any development begins, the wireframes need to be put in front of real congregants — particularly young adults and religious leaders — to validate or revise the core assumptions.

Reflection

I never finished what I started.

In 2022, I designed a study because something troubled me — what happens to community when you take away the physical space it lives in. I wrote the proposal, built the methodology, sat with the questions I wanted to ask 72 people. Then the semester ended, and the interviews never happened. I submitted the paper, got my mark, and moved on.

A few years later, I came back to that proposal with different eyes. I'd been learning UX design, studying human-computer interaction, building a portfolio. I looked at the questions I'd written in 2022 and realised they were good UX research questions — I just never got to ask them.

I built Kopanang on the strength of that proposal and my own experience growing up in the Roman Catholic Church in Botswana, because I think the assumptions underneath it are reasonable ones. But I want to be honest about what this project actually is: a well-designed proposal, a design concept built on informed assumptions, and a clear, unfinished next step. The most useful thing I can do with this project now isn't more design polish — it's finally going to do the interviews.
