The Mbappe Mirror

On mythology, institutional failure, and what happens when a player is handed a distorting mirror at seventeen and never asked to put it down

There is a version of the Kylian Mbappe story that is very easy to tell. Gifted player. Enormous ego. Believed he was bigger than every institution he passed through. Left PSG, went to Real Madrid, everything fell apart. The end. That version is satisfying in the way that morality tales always are: clean, causal, someone to blame. It is also, I think, fundamentally dishonest. Because it locates the failure entirely inside one person while ignoring the system of mirrors that surrounded him from the moment he was seventeen years old and the world decided, collectively and without his meaningful consent, that he was not merely a footballer but a phenomenon unto himself. You cannot hand someone a distorting mirror at the beginning of their adult life, reward every reflection it produces, and then express surprise at what they see when they look into it twenty years later.

What I want to do here is something more uncomfortable than blame. I want to use Mbappe as a case study, at a micro level, as an individual whose psychology was shaped by extraordinary circumstances, and at a macro level as a symptom of what happens when modern football's financial architecture, institutional culture, and mythology-making machinery all converge on a single human being. Because what is happening at Real Madrid right now is not simply a story about one difficult player. It is a story about two broken systems meeting each other, and the slow-motion consequences of that collision.

What the numbers cannot explain

Let me be precise about something before I go further, because I think it is important. Kylian Mbappe is, by any objective measure, an exceptional footballer. He has scored 85 goals in 100 appearances for Real Madrid. This season alone he has produced 41 goals in 41 matches, numbers that would place virtually any other player in the world beyond serious criticism. The argument I am making is not that he has failed on the pitch. The individual statistics do not support that claim, and I will not make it.

What I am arguing is something different: that individual brilliance, uncoupled from collective investment and institutional accountability, is insufficient, and in some configurations actively corrosive. And that the conditions which produced this specific uncoupling were not accidents. They were constructed, over years, by the football industry itself, by PSG's ownership, and by the broader culture of superstar economics that has come to define the modern game. Mbappe is the most visible consequence of that construction. He is not its sole author.

The machine that built the myth

To understand where Mbappe is in May 2026, you have to go back to May 2022, and to one of the most structurally revealing moments in recent football history. Mbappe was twenty-three years old. He had been at PSG for five years and was the best player in the world by almost any metric. Real Madrid wanted him desperately. PSG, facing the prospect of losing their prized asset on a free transfer, made him an offer that went considerably beyond money.

When Mbappe signed his contract extension that summer, he was not signing as a player. He was signing as something closer to a co-director. He was granted effective input over coaching appointments, over player recruitment, over the sporting direction of the club. The sporting director who had a poor relationship with his camp was removed. The club president stood beside him on the pitch at Parc des Princes and declared publicly that Mbappe would become the cornerstone of PSG's project both on and off the pitch. A French football club, backed by Qatari sovereign wealth, handed a twenty-three-year-old the institutional architecture of the organisation and asked him to stay.

The sociologist Émile Durkheim wrote about anomie, which is the condition that arises when the social norms and regulatory structures that govern individual behaviour are removed or overridden. Durkheim's concern was primarily with what happens to societies when their frameworks of collective expectation collapse. But the concept translates precisely to organisations, and to individuals within them. When the normal constraints of institutional membership are suspended for one person, when the rules, the hierarchies, the accountability structures that apply to everyone else are explicitly waived, you do not simply give that person freedom. You remove the structure through which they might have developed a stable relationship with the collective. You create, in Durkheim's terms, a condition of normlessness. And normlessness, for Durkheim, was not freedom. It was a form of psychological and social danger.

PSG did not give Mbappe power because they believed it was good for him. They did it because they were desperate to keep him, and because their entire project, the Galáctico spending, the Messi and Neymar signings, the years of financial excess in pursuit of a Champions League that kept eluding them, had reduced their leverage to almost nothing. They were not building Mbappe. They were accommodating him, which is an entirely different thing. And in doing so, they confirmed for him something that the world had been suggesting for years: that the normal rules of collective life did not apply to him.

Luis Enrique and the mirror

What makes the Luis Enrique chapter so revealing is that it represents the one moment in this entire story where someone with institutional authority looked at Mbappe directly and told him, clearly and without diplomatic softening, that his myth was not enough.

The footage of their one-on-one exchange, which has resurfaced repeatedly since Mbappe's Madrid struggles began, is extraordinary for what it reveals about how Enrique understood the problem. He did not challenge Mbappe's talent. He explicitly affirmed it: "attacking, I already know you are a god, there is no one like you." What he challenged was something more fundamental: the idea that talent alone constitutes leadership. "Michael Jordan would grab his teammates by the balls and start defending with them like a son of a b****," he told the forward. "The day you attack and you have to be the best player in history defending — that's a leader."

This is, in the language of organisational psychology, a challenge to Mbappe's self-concept. The psychologist Carl Rogers distinguished between the self-concept, how we understand ourselves, and the ideal self, how we believe we should be. When these two are in significant tension, the result is anxiety and resistance. Enrique was not simply asking Mbappe to press harder. He was asking him to revise a self-concept that had been built and reinforced over a decade of being treated as singular and exceptional. He was asking him to understand himself as a component of something larger. That is not a tactical adjustment. It is an identity confrontation.

Mbappe's response, in the immediate term, was positive. PSG won the return leg against Barcelona convincingly, with him scoring twice. But the deeper revision Enrique was calling for did not take hold. And when Mbappe left for Madrid the following summer, Enrique said something that deserves to be read as institutional analysis rather than personal grievance: "When a player goes where he wants, that means there are parts of our play that I don't control. And next year I am going to control all of them. Without exception." What followed was PSG winning the Champions League that had eluded them throughout the Mbappe era. The trophy that Messi, Neymar, and all the Qatari billions had failed to deliver. They won it without him.

Talent alone does not constitute leadership. And a system that treats it as though it does produces something that looks like excellence and functions like fragility.

The superstar economics problem

The economist Sherwin Rosen, writing in 1981, described what he called the economics of superstars, which is the tendency in certain markets for rewards to concentrate dramatically at the very top, producing enormous returns for a small number of individuals whose marginal superiority over their peers is vastly disproportionate to their reward differential. Rosen was writing about entertainment markets broadly, but his framework anticipates modern football almost precisely. The market for elite footballers has produced a small number of individuals whose economic value to their clubs is so extreme that it distorts the normal functioning of the organisation around them.

This distortion is not neutral. Research in sports psychology and organisational behaviour consistently shows that teams built around individual brilliance, what researchers call star-centric rather than system-centric structures, tend to underperform relative to their talent level, particularly under pressure, and are disproportionately vulnerable to individual variance. The PSG of Messi, Neymar, and Mbappe is perhaps the cleanest empirical illustration of this in football history. Three of the most gifted players of their generation, assembled at enormous expense, repeatedly failing to produce collective coherence at the highest level. The whole was consistently less than the sum of its extraordinary parts.

What PSG's post-Mbappe UCL victory demonstrated, and what Luis Enrique understood intuitively, is that system coherence, collective accountability, and shared tactical identity are not obstacles to winning. They are preconditions for it. A team of well-integrated good players will, under sufficient pressure, consistently outperform a collection of poorly integrated exceptional ones. This is not a romantic argument about team spirit. It is a structural one, supported by the evidence of what actually happened when the mythology was removed and the machine was allowed to function as a collective.

Real Madrid and the culture Florentino built

Here is where I want to be careful, because this is the dimension of the argument that is most easily misread. The difficulties at Real Madrid since Mbappe's arrival are real and documented. Two consecutive seasons without a major trophy. A dressing room described in reports as toxic and divided. A training ground dispute between Mbappe and a member of the coaching staff. A vacation trip to Italy while teammates played El Clásico. A petition, inflated by bots and unverified signatures, but staggering in its symbolic scale — calling for his departure. These things are not invented.

But to say that Mbappe is the cause of Real Madrid's current condition would be both unfair and analytically lazy. Because the condition of that dressing room was not created by his arrival. It was created over years, by an institutional culture that Florentino Pérez built deliberately and at enormous cost: a culture in which the star is the project, in which individual brilliance is the organising principle, and in which the normal accountability structures of collective professional life are routinely subordinated to the demands of marquee names.

The Xabi Alonso situation — what 233 days revealed

When Xabi Alonso arrived as Real Madrid manager in June 2025, he came as one of the most respected coaching minds in European football, fresh from an extraordinary tenure at Bayer Leverkusen. He came with clear ideas. He wanted to improve punctuality. He wanted to reduce the presence of outside figures at the training ground. He wanted to implement a high-pressing, structured style that required collective sacrifice from individual stars. Basic institutional discipline, applied consistently.

It was not well received. Tensions came to a head when Vinícius Júnior reacted furiously to being substituted during a Clásico. The club declined to fine him. According to multiple sources, that was the moment Alonso definitively lost the dressing room, not because of a tactical failure, but because the institution chose the star over the system, as it had always done. Alonso was sacked 233 days into his tenure, less than 24 hours after a Supercopa defeat to Barcelona.

The detail that matters most for this argument: among the players who actually supported Alonso and believed in his approach was Mbappe. The player most publicly associated with the club's dysfunction was, in this specific instance, on the side of institutional accountability. The picture, as always, is more complicated than the headline.

This is the collision I referred to at the beginning. Mbappe arrived at Real Madrid already shaped by PSG's version of the superstar-above-institution pathology. He arrived into a club that had its own version of the same pathology, with deeper roots and more entrenched power structures. Two systems that had each, in their own way, spent years confirming to their most valuable players that the collective existed to serve them, not the other way around. When those two systems met, the result was not a single player's failure. It was the inevitable consequence of a culture that had been building for a very long time, now fully visible.

The psychology of the pedestal

I do not want to leave Mbappe the person out of this, because the psychological dimension matters and it is distinct from the institutional one. There is a well-documented phenomenon in developmental psychology around what happens to young people who receive extraordinary early success and the kind of identity-defining public attention that Mbappe has experienced since his mid-teens. The concept of self-concept clarity, which is the degree to which a person's understanding of themselves is stable, consistent, and clearly defined, is relevant here. Research consistently shows that self-concept clarity is disrupted, not enhanced, by extreme external validation during the identity-formation years. When your sense of self is built primarily on the reflection you receive from others, the crowd, the media, the club president standing beside you on the pitch — rather than on internal consistency and self-knowledge, that self becomes dependent on the continuation of the reflection. Remove the reflection, or change what it shows, and the identity becomes unstable.

Mbappe has been told since he was a teenager that he is singular. That the rules are different for him. That his presence alone changes the calculus of whatever institution he inhabits. These messages came not from delusion but from the world, from coaches, presidents, journalists, fans, and the financial structures of the game itself. The tragedy, if we want to call it that, is not that he believed them. It is that he was never meaningfully given the opportunity not to. The few voices that tried, Luis Enrique most clearly, were working against enormous structural momentum, and the institutions behind them were simultaneously undermining the message with their actions.

In twenty years, when Mbappe has retired and the distance allows for honest reflection, I suspect the account he gives of this period will be more interesting and more self-aware than the current discourse allows for. Because the evidence suggests he is not without self-knowledge. His support for Alonso's disciplinary project at Madrid, against the grain of more powerful voices in that dressing room, suggests a person who at some level understands what good institutional culture looks like, even if he has not consistently embodied it. The gap between knowing and being able to act is one of the central tensions of human development. It is not unique to footballers. It is simply more visible in them.

You cannot hand someone a distorting mirror at the beginning of their adult life, reward every reflection it produces, and then express surprise at what they see twenty years later.

What the PSG vindication actually proves

The most poetically satisfying element of this entire saga, and I think it is worth sitting with rather than rushing past, is the timing. The same summer Mbappe departed for the dream he had pursued since adolescence, PSG won the Champions League. The trophy that Messi could not deliver. That Neymar could not deliver. That Mbappe himself, for all his brilliance, had not delivered. They won it with a coherent collective, a manager in full control of his squad, and a culture of shared accountability that had been explicitly impossible to build while the superstar exception remained in place.

This is not a coincidence to be explained away. It is a data point. And it is one that the superstar economics model of football, the Galáctico philosophy, the Qatar-funded arms race, the belief that assembling the most individually exceptional players will inevitably produce the most collectively successful team, cannot comfortably accommodate. The PSG that won the UCL was not built on exception. It was built on system. And the system became possible only after the exception left.

Meanwhile at Madrid, the trophies that define the club's identity have not arrived. The dressing room has fractured along multiple fault lines, with physical altercations between players reported in training. A manager who came in with the full weight of his Leverkusen achievement behind him lasted less than a year because the culture he was trying to build was incompatible with the culture the institution had spent decades constructing. And the player at the centre of the public narrative, 85 goals in 100 games, individually productive, collectively insufficient — is the subject of a petition that has gathered tens of millions of signatures regardless of its dubious technical validity, because the symbolic gesture of that many people saying something has gone wrong is itself meaningful data.

The cautionary tale that was always coming

I want to end where I began, with the question of responsibility. It would be easy, and wrong, to place it solely at Mbappe's feet. It would be equally wrong to absolve him entirely on the grounds that the system failed him. Both things can be true simultaneously, that an individual is shaped by conditions they did not choose, and that at a certain point of adult agency, they bear some accountability for whether they interrogate those conditions or simply continue to inhabit them.

What Mbappe's career to this point represents, I think, is a cautionary tale not about ego in isolation but about the structural conditions that produce unchecked ego and then punish it, often in the same institutions that created it. PSG built a player-king and then acted surprised when the king was difficult to govern. Real Madrid built a culture of superstar entitlement over decades and then sacked the coach who tried to dismantle it from within. And Mbappe, the most visible beneficiary of both systems, is now the most visible casualty of their contradictions.

He is twenty-seven years old. He has his whole career ahead of him and a contract at Madrid until 2029. The World Cup in the United States this summer will provide, as it always does, a reset of narrative, a context in which individual brilliance can shine without the friction of club institutional politics. Whatever happens there, the deeper questions his story raises will not be resolved by a tournament. They will only be resolved if someone, the player, the club, the industry, is willing to look at what the mirror actually shows, rather than what they have spent years believing it should.

No one can deny what Kylian Mbappe can do with a football. The records are real. The talent is real. What his story asks us to examine is whether talent, in the absence of the structures that make collective excellence possible, is sufficient. The evidence, from Paris and from Madrid, suggests it is not. And the institutions that built him, that handed him the mirror and told him it showed the future of football — bear as much responsibility for that answer as he does.
