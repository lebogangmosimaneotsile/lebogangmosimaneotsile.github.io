Their Bodies, Our Business

On fan entitlement, racialised surveillance, and the athlete who is never simply allowed to just be

In December, I got into an argument, or something similar to one, with a friend. They were questioning why a South African footballer had been spotted at a HomeComing event. The implication was clear: he shouldn't be there. He should be elsewhere, doing something more befitting of what is expected from an athlete. My response, and another friend's response, was simple. He is a person. He is unwinding. He is simply doing what other people do.

This wasn't the first time this friend had said something like this, and as it turned out, it wasn't the last. A few months earlier, a different South African footballer had been seen at Tempo, and the same energy surfaced, the same quiet indignation, the same sense that something improper had occurred. Then, just this past weekend, the comment came again. A South African footballer was spotted at Tempo after a poor performance, and the response, shared with me by the same friend, read simply: he dropped a 0/10. Why is he at Tempo?

That same weekend, a photo of Jack Grealish circulated online appearing to show him heavily intoxicated at a social event. Predictably, two conversations happened simultaneously. One was about Grealish. The other was about what would have happened if Grealish were Black.

I want to sit in that second conversation for a bit, because I think it reveals something that is a lot more insidious than a photo or a footballer at a club. But first, I want to stay with the comment about Tempo for a moment, because there is something in it that unlocks the whole argument. The comment is not simply "why is he out." It is: he dropped a 0/10. Why is he at Tempo? The performance rating and the question about his location are presented as inseparable, as though one follows logically from the other. Poor game, therefore he forfeits the right to be at Tempo. Poor game, therefore his Saturday night is no longer his own. That is not a moral argument. That is not about role models or children or the integrity of the sport. It is a claim of ownership, stated plainly. You underperformed, so your body owes us something. And that debt is paid not just on the pitch, but in the hours after it and the days beyond. That is the logic I want to argue.

The transaction we keep misreading

When you buy a ticket, stream a match, or purchase a jersey, you are buying access to athletic labour. You are paying for what happens on the pitch. That is the transaction. That is the entirety of what was agreed upon.

No professional in any other field is held to a different standard. If you are a client at KPMG and your analyst delivers poor work, you raise it. You escalate. You may even leave. But if that analyst is photographed at a restaurant on a Saturday night having a drink with friends, the thought that you are owed an explanation would not cross your mind. The work and the person are understood to be two separate things. We extend that basic courtesy to almost every profession without thinking about it. We do not extend it to athletes.

And this is worth pausing on, because athletes are not the only people who earn their living through their bodies. Actors perform with theirs. Singers do. Dancers do. A pop star photographed leaving a club is considered humanising, even glamorous. An actor photographed drunk at a party becomes a tabloid moment that fades by Tuesday. But an athlete photographed in the same scenario becomes a disciplinary problem, a character indictment, evidence of something gone wrong. The question is why the body of the athlete is held to a standard the body of the artist is not. And a lot of the time, the said athlete is not even drunk. They are simply in a social setting that you have decided they have no business being in.

At least a few things are producing this. First, there is what we might call the illusion of unmediated access. We watch athletes in real time, under genuine pressure, with no editing and no second takes. The actor's performance is shaped in post-production. The athlete's is raw and immediate. This creates in the viewer a false sense of intimacy, a feeling that because you have witnessed the body struggling and triumphing in real time, you know it. That proximity breeds a possessiveness that artistic performance rarely generates in the same way.

Second, there is collective identity. Athletes, uniquely, represent collectives: a club, a city, a nation. When Rashford plays for England, millions of people feel they have personal investment in what his body does. That communal stake generates communal entitlement. The pop star represents herself. The athlete represents us. And we have decided, without ever asking the athlete, that this representation is total, extending beyond the final whistle into the rest of their life.

Third, athletic bodies are culturally coded as instruments of productive labour in a way that artistic bodies are not. The artist's off-stage persona is understood as part of the creative identity. The chaos, the excess, the contradiction are all legible as artistic temperament. The athlete's off-pitch persona is framed purely as a distraction from performance. Which means any deviation from expected conduct reads not as personality, but as negligence. The body exists to perform. When it is not performing, it should be preparing to perform. Rest, pleasure, ordinary human living. These are tolerated at best and suspect at worst. And if the performance has been poor? Then the body is in debt, and a night out is an insult to the creditor.

The parasocial trap

Psychology gives us useful language here. Researchers have described what are called parasocial relationships, which are the one-sided emotional bonds that people form with public figures through media exposure. These bonds are not metaphorical. They elicit genuine emotional responses. They produce real feelings of loyalty, pride, and yes, betrayal.

The problem is that parasocial relationships are entirely fictitious on one side. The athlete has not agreed to be your friend. They have not agreed to be your family member or your moral compass. All they have agreed to do is play football. But because the fan's emotional investment feels real to the fan, the entitlement that follows also feels justified. It does not feel like overreach. It feels like a reasonable response to being let down by someone they care about.

This is why the 0/10 comment reads the way it does. The person writing it is not performing outrage for an audience. They genuinely feel that the footballer's presence at Tempo is a kind of affront, because somewhere in the architecture of parasocial investment, that footballer has become someone who owes them something. The debt is emotional before it is rational. And like all emotional debts, it is very difficult to argue someone out of it.

This psychological dynamic would be complicated enough on its own. But then you layer race on top of it, and everything shifts.

Two scandals, one standard

Marcus Rashford organised and campaigned for a school meals programme that fed children across the United Kingdom during a period when the government had chosen not to fund it. The response from significant parts of the English media and public was suspicion. Cynicism. An implication that his motivations were performative, political, self-serving. A young Black man feeding children became a controversy.

Raheem Sterling bought his mother a house. A son used his earnings to provide for the woman who raised him. The tabloid coverage framed it as evidence of financial recklessness, and this was before Sterling had done anything remotely controversial. The act of spending his own money, on his own mother, in his own time, was treated as public business. A character indictment dressed up as concern.

Jack Grealish is photographed unconscious from alcohol that same weekend a South African footballer is questioned for simply being at a club. And the conversation around Grealish, for the most part, is gentle. He's just being a guy. He works hard. Let him live. And to be fair, at the time that picture was taken, he was injured. But even that should not matter to this argument, because it would still be his body and his weekend either way.

The Rashford sequence — what the timeline reveals

In late November 2024, Rashford was allegedly spotted out in Manchester the evening before a match. His camp denied the claims, and United dealt with it internally. What happened next is the part that matters: he went out and scored twice in a 4-0 demolition of Everton, a performance so commanding it was described at the time as a blueprint for the Amorim era at Old Trafford.

It turned out to be his last league start for the club. He was progressively frozen out over the following weeks, loaned to Aston Villa in January, and is now at Barcelona. The punishment came and persisted despite the performance, not because of poor performance. Which tells you that the performance was never really the point.

I want to be precise here because precision matters. This is not an argument that Grealish should have been treated more harshly. There is nothing inherently wrong with getting drunk. My point is not that he deserved punishment. My point is that Rashford and Sterling were doing genuinely good things and were punished anyway, while Grealish was doing something ordinary and was largely excused, while a Black South African footballer being at a club on a weekend is treated as a scandal requiring three question marks, as we saw in that comment. The moral scrutiny is not being applied consistently. It is being applied selectively. And the selection follows a clear racial logic.

The sociologist Stuart Hall wrote about how Black subjects in Western media rarely occupy the middle ground of ordinary humanity. Representation tends to oscillate between two poles: the exceptional and the deviant. The inspiring story or the cautionary tale. What this means in practice is that Black athletes are almost never allowed to simply exist. Rashford cannot feed children without it becoming a political statement. Sterling cannot provide for his family without it becoming some kind of financial warning. A South African footballer cannot be at Tempo, for argument's sake, without it becoming evidence of misplaced priorities. The ordinary is not a luxury available to them. And when they do something that would read as unremarkable by anyone else's standard: having a drink, going to a club, being seen at a HomeComing event — it reads instead as transgression. The middle ground that Grealish occupies comfortably is simply not on offer.

The role model argument, and why it doesn't hold

Someone will always raise the role model argument. He has a responsibility. Children are watching. He chose a public life. I want to take this seriously rather than dismiss it, because there is something there, and because the way it collapses under examination is itself revealing.

When you are visible at the scale that Premier League footballers are visible, you will inevitably become a reference point for young people. That is true. But there is a significant difference between a child looking up to an athlete's skill, their dedication, their journey, versus a parent outsourcing their child's entire moral education to someone who plays football for a living. Jack Grealish, to the best of my knowledge, has never positioned himself as a moral guide to children. He has never applied for that job. The role model designation was assigned to him by a public that then holds him accountable to a job description he never accepted.

More importantly, the role model argument is almost never applied as a principle. It is applied as a weapon. It gets invoked when someone wants to punish a specific behaviour, not as a genuine ethical framework. If it were a consistent principle, it would be applied evenly. As we know, it is not. Grealish's photograph did not produce widespread concern about what message he is sending to children. The South African footballer at Tempo did not prompt anyone to ask about role models either. The comment was not "think about the children." It was: he dropped a 0/10. The role model framing is a post-hoc justification, not a guiding principle. It gets layered on top of a judgment that was already made for other reasons.

Diego Maradona is perhaps the most instructive example. Maradona struggled publicly and deeply with cocaine addiction throughout and beyond his playing career. It was not a secret. It was widely known and widely reported. And yet he remains one of the most beloved footballers in the history of the sport. People held his football and his humanity in the same hand and chose to honour the former without demanding the latter be perfect. They were capable of that nuance when they wanted to be. Which means the capacity has always existed. The question is when we choose to deploy it, and for whom.

The longer history of this entitlement

I cannot write about the policing of Black athletes' bodies without acknowledging where that impulse comes from historically, because it does not emerge from nowhere. It is not a product of social media culture or the twenty-four hour news cycle. It has roots that are considerably older and considerably darker.

Frantz Fanon wrote in Black Skin, White Masks about how the Black body is never fully one's own within a society structured by the white gaze. It is always already being interpreted, surveilled, assigned meaning by others before it has had a chance to assign meaning to itself. The Black body in public space is never simply present. It is always being read. And the readings arrive loaded with centuries of projection, fear, and instrumentalisation.

The Black athlete exists at a particular intensification of this dynamic. Their body is celebrated as exceptional, faster, stronger, more gifted, while simultaneously being surveilled as suspect the moment it steps outside the designated space of that exceptionalism. On the pitch, the Black body is an asset. At a club on a Saturday night, at a HomeComing event, in a photograph taken and circulated without consent, it becomes a problem that requires explaining.

The theorist bell hooks wrote extensively about how Black culture and Black bodies are consumed by mainstream audiences for entertainment and profit, while the actual humanity of Black people remains systematically devalued. The Black athlete sits at the sharpest point of this contradiction. Their talent, their physical ability, their charisma, all of it is monetised and consumed. Their jersey is purchased. Their highlight is streamed. Their celebration is imitated in living rooms. But the consumption has limits. It stops at the boundary of their personhood. You are welcome to profit off Rashford's left foot. You are not required to extend him the full dignity of a human being who gets tired, who wants to celebrate, who occasionally has a drink, who dropped a 0/10 on Saturday and still gets to exist on Saturday night.

The sociologist Orlando Patterson introduced the concept of social death to describe the condition of the enslaved: existing outside social recognition, having one's will and personhood nullified, existing entirely in relation to someone else's needs and purposes. Please do not misunderstand me. I am not arguing that modern athletes are enslaved. That would be a reductive claim and an easy one to dismiss. What I am arguing is that the logic persists in diluted but recognisable form: the idea that certain bodies exist for the use, entertainment, and approval of others, and that those bodies stepping outside their assigned purpose constitutes a transgression.

When my friend questioned why a South African footballer was at a HomeComing event, and then months later questioned why another was at Tempo after a poor performance, the underlying question both times, stripped of its social niceties, was the same: why are you somewhere I didn't place you? That question has been asked of Black people, in various registers of violence and condescension, for a very long time. The register has changed. The question has not.

Parents, surrogates, and the outsourced moral economy

There is one more dimension that I think is underexamined, and it connects the individual psychology to a broader social pattern. The role model demand placed on athletes is not simply about athletes. It is about a quiet crisis in moral education that has been offloaded onto public figures without their knowledge or consent.

The kind of moral formation that used to happen through extended family structures, through religious institutions, through coaches and teachers known personally, has eroded significantly in the social media era. In its place, children are forming their values in relation to figures they have never met, who have never met them, and who became visible not because of their moral wisdom but because of what they can do with a football. Parents, sometimes without realising it, have outsourced a function that requires relationship and proximity to people who can only offer spectacle.

When a footballer goes to Tempo after a poor performance, some people feel that something has been taken from them. Not because that footballer has wronged anyone. But because they had unconsciously deputised him to do a job he never applied for: to be disciplined, aspirational, self-punishing on their behalf — and his presence at a club on a Saturday night reveals that he was never doing it. The outrage is partly the outrage of someone whose subcontractor has walked off a job they were never formally offered.

This dynamic would be unfair applied to any athlete. Applied along racial lines, it becomes an additional burden laid specifically on Black athletes, who are already navigating heightened scrutiny, racialised representation, and the condition Fanon described, to also serve as the moral infrastructure for communities that have decided their excellence obligates them to total self-sacrifice.

What we are really asserting

When we photograph an athlete in a private moment and circulate it as evidence of something, when we debate whether a footballer deserves to be at a restaurant or a club or a HomeComing event, when we rate a player 0/10 and in the same breath question why he is seen out, we are making a claim. We are saying that their time is not their own. That their bodies are not their own. That they exist, fundamentally, in relation to us and our needs. And that a poor performance does not just mean we are disappointed in what they did on the pitch. It means we are owed their weekend too.

This entitlement would be troubling applied to anyone. Applied unevenly along racial lines, and the evidence that it is applied unevenly is consistent and overwhelming, it becomes something more serious. It becomes a continuation, softened and mediated through social media and sports journalism, of the idea that Black excellence is on loan. That it can be celebrated when it serves us and scrutinised when it does not. That the person attached to the talent is, at best, a secondary concern.

Grealish blacked out at a party. Rashford fed children. Sterling provided for his mother. One South African footballer went to a HomeComing event, and another had a night out, and both were made to feel, by the same person, that their presence required explanation. All of this happened, some of it in the same weekend. The fact that the scrutiny consistently lands on Black bodies in ordinary spaces doing ordinary things does not require elaborate explanation. It requires only that we are willing to look clearly at the pattern and be honest enough to call it for what it is.

Their bodies are their own. They always were. Every second they spend on a pitch is labour offered in exchange for compensation agreed upon by contract. Every second they spend off it belongs entirely to them, whether they scored a hat-trick or dropped a stinker. The sooner we internalise that, not as a vague aspiration but as a structural fact, the more honest our relationship with sport, with race, and with each other will become.
