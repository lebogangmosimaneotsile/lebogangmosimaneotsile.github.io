What the Mirror Reflects Now

On Mbappe's legacy, PSG's back-to-back UCL titles, and the teammate nobody saw coming

This is a follow-up to The Mbappe Mirror, written earlier this year. That piece examined how two dysfunctional systems, PSG and Real Madrid, shaped and then collided with Mbappe's ego and sense of self. This piece picks up where that one left off, because the story has developed in ways that deserve a response.

This past Saturday, PSG beat Arsenal on penalties in Budapest to win the Champions League for the second consecutive year. Back to back. The trophy that eluded them through all those years of excess, through Neymar, through the Messi experiment, through the era defined entirely by Kylian Mbappe, they have now won it twice in a row without him. Meanwhile, Real Madrid have gone trophyless in back-to-back seasons since his arrival. I wrote in the previous piece about the institutional collision that produced this outcome. What I want to examine now is what it means for the legacy of the player himself.

Because legacy is complicated, and I want to be careful with it.

The club legacy, plainly stated

Let us just look at the facts as they stand. Since Mbappe left PSG at the end of the 2023/24 season, the club has won back-to-back Champions League titles and Ousmane Dembélé has won the Ballon d'Or. Since Mbappe arrived at Real Madrid, the club has won nothing of significance. In his debut 2024/25 season he scored 43 goals across all competitions, breaking Real Madrid's record for goals in a debut season. This season he has been La Liga's top scorer and the Champions League's leading scorer with 15 goals in the competition. The individual numbers are, by any measure, extraordinary. And yet the trophies have not come, the dressing room has fractured, and a petition calling for his departure gathered tens of millions of signatures within days.

I am not going to make the argument that Mbappe is solely responsible for Madrid's struggles, because that would be intellectually lazy and factually weak. Madrid's problems predate him. The institutional culture Florentino Pérez built, where star players feel larger than the system, was already well established before Mbappe arrived. As I argued in the previous piece, two dysfunctional systems met each other, and the result was inevitable.

But I will say this. The correlation is striking enough to take seriously. Not as proof of causation, but as a question worth sitting with. What is it about the spaces Mbappe occupies that seems to produce this pattern?

The international legacy, honestly assessed

The defence of Mbappe, when his club record comes up, is almost always the same. Look at what he has done with France. And there is something there. He won the World Cup in 2018 as a teenager, which was a genuinely historic achievement and one nobody should diminish. But I want to look at the fuller picture, because I think the fuller picture is more instructive than the headline.

In 2022, France reached the World Cup final. They lost to Argentina. Mbappe scored a hattrick in that final, which sounds extraordinary until you look at the composition of it. Two of those three goals were penalties. A hat-trick built primarily on spot kicks, in a game his team lost, is a more complicated legacy point than the number alone suggests. I am not saying penalties do not count. I am saying that the mythology being built around that performance deserves some scrutiny. Scoring twice from the spot in a losing final is not the same as dragging your team to victory.

Then at the Euros, France lost to Spain. Another final, another defeat.

So the international record reads: one World Cup win in 2018, two subsequent finals reached and lost. And I find myself asking the same question I asked about the club record. What is it about the biggest moments that produces this pattern? I want to be careful here, because France is a collective and their wins and losses cannot be placed at the feet of any individual. But patterns are worth examining. And the pattern across both his club career and his international career is one of arriving at the final moment and falling short of it.

The mythology being built around that 2022 performance deserves scrutiny. Scoring twice from the spot in a losing final is not the same as dragging your team to victory.

The problem with the team sport caveat

Here is where I want to complicate my own argument, because I think intellectual honesty requires it.

When people point to PSG's UCL wins as evidence against Mbappe, the response is often: football is a team sport. You cannot credit or blame one player for collective outcomes. And that is correct. PSG won without him because Luis Enrique built something coherent, because Dembélé found his best form, because the institution finally prioritised system over superstar. That is a collective achievement.

But this cuts both ways. If we cannot blame Mbappe for PSG's previous UCL failures or Madrid's current struggles, then we also cannot fully credit him for France's 2018 World Cup win. If football is a team sport when it is convenient to defend him, it must also be a team sport when it is convenient to praise him. You cannot have it both ways. The 2018 World Cup win belongs to that French team. Mbappe was a part of it, a spectacular part, but so was Griezmann, so was Pogba, so was a defensive unit that barely conceded. The individual mythology requires a selective reading of collective events.

What I am left with, when I try to be as fair as I can, is this. Mbappe is a generational talent. His numbers are real. But his legacy, at this point in his career, is thinner than the mythology suggests. One World Cup winner's medal earned as part of a very good team. A 2022 final hattrick that comprises two penalties in a game France lost. A club record at Madrid that is individually impressive and collectively barren. And a former club that has won everything the moment he stopped being the centre of their universe.

The person nobody was watching

Now we get to the part of the story that I find most interesting, and most telling.

When Mbappe was being positioned as the successor to Messi and Ronaldo, the conversation was relatively narrow. Who would be the next one? The general consensus pointed toward Mbappe. He was fast, prolific, French, young, and marketable in exactly the way the Ballon d'Or ecosystem rewards. The next era had a face, and that face was his.

Nobody was talking about Ousmane Dembélé.

Dembélé's career to that point had been defined almost entirely by what he had not achieved. A record transfer to Barcelona that never quite worked out. Persistent injury problems. A reputation for being difficult, unreliable, wasteful of his obvious talent. He was the cautionary tale playing out in the shadow of the designated superstar. When he moved to PSG in 2023, it felt like a reset for a career that had stalled, not the beginning of something historic.

The Dembélé record, since Mbappe's departure

In the 2024-25 season, Dembélé scored 37 goals and added 15 assists as PSG won a historic treble. He won the Champions League Player of the Season award and then, in September 2025, the Ballon d'Or. He finished ahead of Lamine Yamal and his own PSG teammates to claim the award that Mbappe, by most predictions, was destined to win.

In 2025/26, he played an integral part in PSG's second consecutive UCL title, scoring the equalising penalty in the final against Arsenal. He is now a back-to-back European champion and a Ballon d'Or winner. He is 28 years old. Mbappe, at the same age, finished seventh in the 2025 Ballon d'Or rankings.

The thing that makes this so pointed is not just that someone else has taken the accolades. It is that the person who took them was not even in the conversation. Dembélé was not positioned as the next great thing. Nobody was building mythologies around him. Luis Enrique converted him into a striker almost as an experiment, and it became a masterstroke. He flourished in a system that demanded collective contribution, that had no space for the kind of ego-driven individualism the previous era had accommodated. He won the Ballon d'Or not because he was anointed, but because he earned it in a context that rewarded the right things.

And Mbappe, the man who was anointed, who was told from the age of seventeen that the future of football had his name on it, is watching all of this from Madrid. Seventh in the Ballon d'Or. Trophyless. Increasingly the subject of public campaigns for his removal. The designated heir to Messi and Ronaldo, replaced in football's imagination not by another designated heir but by someone who simply got on with the work.

What the World Cup adds

The World Cup begins in less than two weeks. France will be among the favourites. Mbappe will almost certainly play a central role, and there is every possibility that he performs well, that France go deep, that the narrative shifts again in his favour. Football has a way of rewriting stories mid-chapter, and I am not going to pretend otherwise.

But here is the added layer that makes this World Cup particularly interesting to watch. If France win the World Cup, and if Dembélé is central to that triumph as he has been central to everything PSG have achieved, he becomes the front runner for a second consecutive Ballon d'Or. Which would mean that the player Mbappe was supposed to succeed, the position in football's hierarchy that was reserved for him, the legacy he has been building toward since he was a teenager, would be occupied instead by his former teammate. A teammate who was, by any reasonable assessment, supposed to be a footnote in Mbappe's story.

That is not just ironic. It is a complete inversion of the expected narrative. Dembélé was never supposed to be the story. He was supposed to be the supporting cast. The fact that he has become the protagonist, in the same team, in the same era, immediately after Mbappe left, says something that no amount of individual goal tallies can answer.

What it says, I think, connects back to everything I argued in the first piece. Systems matter. Collective culture matters. A player who demands the world revolve around him will sometimes produce the numbers. What he will not reliably produce is the thing football actually measures in the end, which is trophies won alongside other people who wanted to be there.

Dembélé wanted to be there. PSG wanted him there. And the mirror that Mbappe has been looking into since he was seventeen is now reflecting someone else's face entirely.

Written on 1 June 2026, days after PSG beat Arsenal on penalties to win the 2025-26 UEFA Champions League, their second consecutive title. Dembélé scored PSG's equaliser from the penalty spot. The 2026 FIFA World Cup begins in the United States later this month. Dembélé won the 2025 Ballon d'Or in September, finishing ahead of Lamine Yamal and Vitinha. Mbappe finished seventh.
