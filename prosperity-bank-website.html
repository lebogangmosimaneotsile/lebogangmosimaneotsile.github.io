Overview

A full responsive banking website designed across desktop and mobile breakpoints, covering the complete UX/UI process: brand identity, design system creation, information architecture, hi-fidelity UI design, and an interactive prototype. Built as a portfolio project to demonstrate end-to-end product design capability.

This website shares its audience and design language with Banking Without Borders — the mobile account-opening experience designed for the same two user groups, Thandi and Graça. Where that project solves the high-stakes moment of opening an account, this site extends the same brand and design system into the everyday browsing, product discovery, and ongoing banking experience around it.

The Brief

One bank. Three audiences.

Design a credible, full-service banking website for a fictional bank, Prosperity Bank, that works seamlessly across desktop and mobile. The site needed to communicate trust, accessibility, and product clarity while serving three distinct customer segments: personal banking clients, business banking clients, and investors.

The challenge wasn't just visual. It required solving real information architecture problems: how do you present a wide product range without overwhelming users? How do you maintain brand consistency across 13+ screens at two breakpoints? And how do you design a system that scales?

Brand Identity

Built as a brand, not a UI exercise.

Prosperity Bank was built from the ground up as a brand. The name was chosen to communicate aspiration and financial growth across all customer segments, and every visual decision traces back to that idea.

Logo

A chevron mark built from four forward-pointing arrows, representing momentum, progress, and forward movement. The repetition of the chevron reinforces consistency and direction.

Typography

Oswald — chosen for its strong, condensed structure that reads with authority at large display sizes while remaining legible at body scale. Used across Bold, SemiBold, Medium, and Regular weights.

Design System

Built before a single screen.

Before designing a single screen, a full design system was built in Figma to ensure consistency across all breakpoints — colour styles, type scales for both desktop and mobile, and a component library covering buttons, inputs, account cards, progress bars, and checkboxes in every state.

Key decision: Desktop and mobile components were kept separate. Mobile components were rebuilt to use auto layout with fill-width behaviour, minimum 48px touch targets, and mobile-appropriate sizing — rather than scaling down desktop components, which caused layout and overflow issues.

Key Design Decisions

Five decisions. Each with a reason.

01

A separate mobile design system

Attempting to reuse desktop components on mobile screens caused consistent layout failures — fixed widths, broken text wrapping, and incorrect touch targets. Building a dedicated mobile component set resolved these issues and is the correct long-term approach for any responsive design system.

02

Gold Rewards loyalty programme

Rather than treating the rewards section as a generic feature, a named loyalty programme — Gold Rewards — was designed with intentional brand logic. Gold directly references the brand's accent colour, making it visually cohesive without explanation, and the name is inclusive and accessible, signalling that rewards are available to all customers.

03

A two-tier loyalty ecosystem

Gold Rewards functions as the entry-level programme for all customers. A second tier — The Prosperity Circle — was conceived as an invitation-only premium tier for wealth management and investment clients. This creates aspiration within the rewards structure: Gold Rewards members have something to work toward. The decision was driven by product thinking, not just visual design.

04

A full-screen nav drawer on mobile

Rather than a standard dropdown, a full-screen navy overlay was used for mobile navigation. This gives each nav item adequate tap target space, removes the visual complexity of overlapping content, and feels native to modern mobile banking apps.

05

A Gold Rewards promotional banner

The bottom banner on content screens was used as a promotional placement for Gold Rewards, replacing a generic image placeholder with purposeful product marketing. This makes the site feel like a real banking product rather than a design exercise.

Accessibility

Designed for everyone.

👁

Colour Contrast

Navy (#00274D) on Off-White (#F5F5F0) meets WCAG AA contrast requirements. Gold on navy was used for decorative and interactive elements only, never for body text.

👆

Touch Targets

All interactive mobile elements were designed at a minimum of 48px height, meeting accessibility standards for users with motor difficulties.

🔤

Typography Legibility

The mobile type scale was reduced thoughtfully, keeping body text at 14px minimum to maintain readability on small screens.

Takeaways

What this project taught me.

What worked well: Building the design system before any screens saved significant time and enforced visual consistency across 13 screens. The brand identity decisions — particularly the chevron logo and navy/gold palette — gave every screen a coherent visual language without needing to make new decisions at each step.

What I learned: Responsive design isn't about scaling — it's about rethinking layout, hierarchy, and interaction patterns for the context a user is operating in. A desktop navbar and a mobile nav drawer aren't the same component at different sizes; they're fundamentally different UX solutions to the same problem.

Reflection

What I'd do differently.

I would define the mobile component library at the same time as the desktop system, rather than after. The decision to build mobile components separately was correct — but making it earlier would have prevented the component issues encountered mid-project.
